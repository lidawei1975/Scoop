## 1. Abstract

This disclosure describes a volumetric measuring scoop engineered to eliminate product waste and dead zones within cylindrical or flat-bottomed containers. Unlike conventional injection-molded scoops that feature rounded, hemispherical, or spherical-tangent bottom bowls, the disclosed scoop features a containment cup with a geometric profile that is a direct three-dimensional complement to the interior corner architecture of a standard cylinder. By incorporating a strict 90° perpendicular interface between its horizontal floor and vertical scraping wall, the scoop maintains continuous surface-to-surface contact with both the floor and vertical side walls of a container simultaneously. This geometry allows a user to mechanically sweep and recover 100% of fine or granulated particulate matter (such as infant formula, protein isolates, or chemical powders) from the bottom perimeter rim of a receptacle.

---

## 2. Technical Field and Problem Description

In consumer packaging, fine materials are frequently distributed in cylindrical plastic tubs or metal canisters featuring a flat horizontal floor meeting a vertical, circular side wall. Standard volumetric scoops provided within these packages are universally manufactured with rounded, convex, or radiused bottom profiles to ease machine ejection during plastic injection molding.

When a conventional rounded scoop enters the lower corner junction of a cylindrical container, the curved profile of the scoop creates a geometric mismatch. The scoop rides up the curved wall or pivots over the floor, leaving an inaccessible ring of compressed particulate matter along the interior circumference. For premium or high-cost powders—such as infant formula, pharmaceuticals, or nutritional supplements—this architectural mismatch results in continuous cumulative material waste and consumer frustration.

## 3. Detailed Description of the Invention and Geometry

The invention comprises a handle attached to a specialized containment cup. The structural novelty lies entirely in the specific geometric properties of the containment cup, which is optimized to function as an ergonomic, multi-surface scraper blade.

### A. The Scoop Cup Geometry

The containment cup is defined by a floor and a surrounding wall architecture featuring three critical geometric constraints:

**The 90° Perpendicular Profile (The Scraping Edge):** The external bottom floor of the scoop cup and the external vertical wall of the scoop cup meet at a precise, crisp 90° perpendicular angle. This forms a sharp, linear plowing edge along the base of the cup.

**The Cylinder-Complementary Base (The Quadrant/Segment):** In the preferred embodiment, the horizontal floor of the scoop cup is shaped as a geometric quadrant (a quarter-circle wedge) or a segment of a circle. The radius of the curved front edge of this quadrant is engineered to match or closely approximate the internal radius of standard cylindrical consumer canisters.

**The Dual-Axis Interface:** The two straight edges of the quarter-circle floor form a right angle on the horizontal plane, while the wall rising from those edges forms a right angle on the vertical plane.


### B. Method of Operation

When inserted into a cylindrical container, the scoop is manipulated so that its flat bottom floor sits flush against the container's bottom floor, while its straight vertical wall sits flush against the container's inner vertical wall.

As the user sweeps the scoop in a circular motion along the interior perimeter of the container, the sharp 90° edge acts as a continuous plow or squeegee. Because the geometry matches the container's interior coordinate lines perfectly, the scoop sweeps the corner entirely clean, forcing 100% of the trapped powder into the open mouth of the scoop cup.

## 4. Alternative Embodiments (Defensive Multi-Mapping)

To prevent competitors from escaping this disclosure via trivial modifications, the following structural variations are explicitly claimed as part of this prior art disclosure:

**Varying Base Shapes:** While a quarter-circle (quadrant) base is preferred for cylindrical containers, the floor of the scoop cup may alternatively be fabricated as a rectangle, a square, a semi-circle, or a polygon, provided that at least one side profile maintains a sharp 90° intersection between the bottom face and a vertical face.

**Handle Placements:** The handle may extend horizontally from the upper rim of the vertical wall, extend vertically upward from the center of the cup, or be integrated into the side wall as a molded grip or finger tab.

**Material Composition:** The scoop may be manufactured out of food-grade plastics (such as Polypropylene, Polyethylene, or PETG), stamped or machined metals (such as stainless steel or aluminum), or flexible elastomers/silicones that deform under light pressure to adapt to minor variations in a container's internal radius.

**Integrated Leveler:** The top rim of the vertical walls may feature a flat, sharp edge to assist the user in leveling off excess powder against the rim of the container.

## Background and Intent

### Why I am sharing this

Twenty years ago, as a parent dealing with expensive infant formula, I was deeply frustrated by a universal design flaw: standard round scoops leave a persistent, wasteful residue at the bottom of containers due to dead zones that cannot be accessed.

### My goal

I want to ensure that this common-sense, cylinder-matching design remains completely free and accessible. My goal is to create permanent "prior art" so that no corporation can ever monopolize this simple yet effective solution.

### A note on prior art

I am an independent citizen and a hobbyist, not a patent attorney. If a similar design or patent already exists in some hidden corner of industrial manufacturing, I sincerely hope this disclosure helps redirect focus to where it belongs—on solving the real problem rather than protecting patents.
